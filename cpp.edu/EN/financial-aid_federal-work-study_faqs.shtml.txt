                                        <h3 class="h6__proxima">The Basics</h3>
                                        <div class="accordion__item--right">
                                            <svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" class="accordion-chevron" fill="none" height="17" viewBox="0 0 29 17" width="29">

                                    <div class="accordion__content" tabindex="0">
                                        <p class="body2"><h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>What is Federal Work Study?</strong></span></h4>
<p>Federal Work Study (FWS) is a need-based federal student aid program that gives you the opportunity to earn money through part-time employment. This program pays a portion of your wages, so this subsidy makes you very desirable to employers! For example, if a department pays you $16.00 hour, it costs their budget a net $4.00/hour. There are three advantages for the work-study student:</p>
<ul>
<li>The subsidy makes you a highly sought-after employee.</li>
<li>FWS earnings do not count against your aid eligibility when you fill out next year's Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA).</li>
<li>FWS gives you the opportunity to earn part of your college costs rather than increase your loans. The less debt you graduate with, the more choices you'll have in life.</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Who is eligible for Federal Work Study jobs?</strong> </span></h4>
<p>Students who receive financial aid from Cal Poly Pomona and have Federal Work Study included as part of their financial aid package are eligible to apply for Federal Work Study jobs. In order to begin earning FWS funds you must have a complete financial aid file (no missing documents) and your financial aid offer must not be a preliminary offer (with estimated items). Check your <a href="https://my.cpp.edu">BroncoDirect Student Center</a> to review your Aid Summary and to make sure there are no missing documents.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Can a Dream Act student receive Federal Work Study?</strong> </span></h4>
<p>The Federal Work Study program is a federal student aid program. As such, Dream Act students are not eligible to receive Federal Work Study. Dream Students can, however, still work as Student Assistants in non FWS positions.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Can Graduate or Credential students receive Federal Work Study?</strong></span></h4>
<p>If you have filed a <a href="https://studentaid.gov/h/apply-for-aid/fafsa" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><strong>FAFSA</strong></a> by the March 2 priority filing deadline, indicated that you are interested in Federal Work Study on the FAFSA application, are eligible to receive federal financial aid and have financial need, you may be eligible for Federal Work Study. Check your <a href="https://my.cpp.edu">BroncoDirect Student Center</a> to review your Aid Summary.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Can I keep working if I'm not enrolled or graduate?</strong> </span></h4>
<p>Unfortunately, you can&#8217;t continue to earn FWS if you are no longer enrolled. If you withdraw, are dismissed, or graduate, your last day to earn FWS funds is the last day of your enrollment. If your employer wants to continue your employment they may choose to do so, but they are responsible for paying you 100% of your earnings without the FWS subsidy. This arrangement would be between you and your employer and any earnings after enrollment will not be FWS eligible. You must immediately notify your employer of changes in your enrollment.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>How can I get the most out of the work-study program?</strong></span></h4>
<p><strong>Big picture</strong>: FWS jobs give you an edge to help pay for college and graduate with less debt. <strong>Bigger picture</strong>: FWS jobs help you develop your resume, network with employers, obtain major/career-related work, and assist you in becoming a savvy, aggressive job hunter.&#160;</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Is Federal Work Study mandatory? Do I have to get a job?</strong></span></h4>
<p>No, you are not required to work. You may have the option of converting your FWS to loans. If you do not use FWS, it is less likely you'll receive it as part of your financial aid the following year. If you don&#8217;t convert your FWS to loans or don&#8217;t earn it, be sure not to include it as a resource in your personal budget.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Do I have to work at a Federal Work Study job?</strong></span></h4>
<p>No, but one of the advantages of work-study is your earnings are not counted as additional income on your Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA). If you work at a non-FWS job, you will need to report your earnings as income on your FAFSA. This is better than not working, but not as good as working at a FWS job.</p></p>

                                        <p class="body2"><h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Am I assigned a job by the Office of Financial Aid &amp; Scholarships?</strong></span></h4>
<p>No. You get to review job openings and apply as you would for any other job. The application process will give you an opportunity to develop your resume, which you&#8217;ll include with your application, along with a cover letter, and it will often include an interview. The employer chooses how students apply and the employer does the hiring through the referral process.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>When can I begin applying for jobs?</strong></span></h4>
<p>Employers typically begin posting available opportunities in July.&#160; You can begin applying for jobs when you can log in to <a href="https://careercenter.cpp.edu/channels/handshake-101/"><strong>Handshake </strong></a>(through the Career Center).&#160; Though you may see Federal Work Study in your Aid Summary in your MyCPP Student Center, if your financial aid offer is "Estimated," your file is incomplete, or you have holds, you won't be able to log in.</p>
<p>Whenever you begin, speed is crucial in the job hunt. Imagine an employer posts the job and the next day receives a resume and cover letter addressing their specific position. Very impressive! Also, do not wait to hear from one employer before applying to other positions. While you're waiting to hear from that employer, those other positions are getting filled.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>When can a spring admit start working in a Federal Work Study position?</strong></span></h4>
<p>Students who begin at Cal Poly Pomona in the spring term can start working in a Federal Work Study position as early as the first day of classes for the spring term.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>When is the last day that I can find a job?</strong></span></h4>
<p>Federal Work Study positions are available throughout the year except between the last day of the spring semester and the first day of the Fall semester.&#160; From mid-August to early September the number of available jobs peak, many with multiple positions. At the beginning of the spring semester, a smaller peak occurs.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>What types of Federal Work Study jobs are available?</strong></span></h4>
<p>There is a huge variety of Federal Work Study jobs, both on and off campus, available. A quick way to see is to sort the job listings by clicking on the link.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Will I find a job relating to my intended major/career?</strong></span></h4>
<p>We encourage you to apply for jobs related to your major or career.&#160; If you are a freshman, we suggest that you not worry about a major-related job in your first year. You could be competing with upperclassmen and grad students, and it is a transition year for you. Your academics come first, so consider less-demanding jobs to get started.&#160;</p>
<p>Consider what works well with your schedule and pays well. Also, think in terms of networking. If you want to work in a particular major-related area, but don't have the required course work yet, see if you can get any kind of job in that department. Do a great job and make contacts. Networking, networking, networking. Those major/career-related jobs will come.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>How do I find a Federal Work Study job?</strong></span></h4>
<p>Review the <a href="https://careercenter.cpp.edu/">Career Center</a>&#160;for information on searching for FWS jobs.</p>
<p><strong>Note:</strong> some students review the "Qualifications" and say, "Oh, they want blah blah. I don't know blah blah. That rules me out." In other words, they focus on reasons <em>not to apply</em>.</p>
<p><strong>Focus on reasons <em>to apply</em></strong>. The employer may be willing to train you in an area where you don't have experience, especially if it says "prefer blah blah experience." Of course, if it states "must have blah blah experience" you'd be right in not applying. Don&#8217;t confuse the term &#8220;prefer" to mean "must have."</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>I have a great six-page resume, and my cover letter tells the employer how much the job will help me. Why haven't I gotten one interview?</strong> </span></h4>
<p>More is not better in the job hunt! Your resume should be one page and your cover letter should not focus on why the job is good for you, but <strong><em>why you are good for the job</em></strong>. You do so by relating your skills and experiences to the job's specific qualifications. And remember, those experiences do not need to be from previous paid jobs; your volunteer, sports, community service, and classroom experiences have given you a wealth of marketable skills! Not sure where to begin? What to cut? The <a href="https://www.cpp.edu/career/index.shtml"><strong>Career Center</strong></a> can help!</p></p>

                                        <p class="body2"><h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>My financial aid is listed as "Estimated." Can I be hired in a Federal Work Study job?</strong></span></h4>
<p>You cannot earn FWS or be referred to a FWS job until the "Estimated" status is removed. &#160;Check your <a href="https://my.cpp.edu">BroncoDirect Student Center</a> to review your Aid Summary and to find out what you need to do to resolve your estimated award.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>My message reads "File Incomplete." Can I be hired in a Federal Work Study job?</strong> </span></h4>
<p>You cannot earn Federal Work Study or be referred to a FWS job until your file is complete.&#160;&#160;Check your <a href="https://my.cpp.edu">BroncoDirect Student Center</a> to review your Aid Summary and to find out what you need to do to&#160; complete your file.</p>
<hr/>
<p>&#160;</p>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Why did my Federal Work Study amount decrease?</strong></span></h4>
<p>Federal Work Study can decrease because financial aid in another area increased, e.g., an outside scholarship check arrived. Your financial aid offer is a zero-sum game; you can't receive more financial aid than you're eligible for.</p>
<p>It may have decreased because you requested that it be converted to a loan.</p>
<p>Or it may have decreased because you withdrew, were dismissed, graduated (students who are not enrolled cannot continue in their work-study position), or lost your eligibility for financial aid due to not meeting the <a href="../resources/understanding-sap.shtml">Satisfactory Academic Progress Standards for Financial Aid Applicants</a>.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>What does my Federal Work Study dollar amount mean?</strong></span></h4>
<p>The amount listed in your Financial Aid Summary for Federal Work Study represents the total amount you can earn in a FWS position (it takes into account both the FWS share and the employer's share.</p>
<p>Your financial aid offer splits the FWS Offer into two equal amounts - one for fall and one for spring but as far as work-study is concerned, you can earn any or all of that amount at any point during the program year (until the last day of finals for the spring semester) while enrolled and as long <em>as you are going to be enrolled for both semesters</em>.</p>
<p>&#160;</p></p>

                                </div>

                                    <button aria-expanded="false" aria-label="Now That I've Been Hired" class="accordion__header" tabindex="0">
                                        <h3 class="h6__proxima">Now That I've Been Hired</h3>
                                        <div class="accordion__item--right">
                                            <svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" class="accordion-chevron" fill="none" height="17" viewBox="0 0 29 17" width="29">

                                        <p class="body2"><h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>How many hours per week do I need to work?</strong></span></h4>
<p>It depends on the hourly wage. For example, if you have $4,000 in work-study for the academic year and you find a $16.50/hour job, figure on three months of solid work for the Fall: September, October, and November to earn $2000. That's approximately $670 per month ($2,000/3), which equals $167.50/week ($670/4), which equals 10.15 hours per week of work ($167.50/$16.50 per hour).</p>
<p>This, by the way, is right in the optimum range of hours to work during the week while going to school. A national study showed that students who work 10-15 hours per week have a higher graduation rate than the overall student body. However, the graduation rate begins to decline as the number of hours worked increases above 15 and students who try to work more than 20 hours per week and go to school full-time have the highest dropout rate.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Once hired, do I have to stay in that job?</strong></span></h4>
<p>You can leave a job. You want to try to do it on good terms, give two weeks&#8217; notice, and never take any displeasure out on your job duties. But if you have to quit on short notice, quit. Do not, however, include that three-day job on your resume or include that supervisor on a list of references (it is highly unlikely you'd get a good reference).</p>
<p>Think in terms of the big picture:&#160; you don't want to wake up in a cold sweat one night fifteen years from now, wondering if your life would be different, if your career would be different, if you'd gotten into a better grad school, if your GPA as an undergrad had been higher, if you'd had a higher grade in that one class, if you had more time to study for the midterm instead of giving in to an unsympathetic employer who demanded that you work. Conversely, employers are not obligated to keep you employed if you are not performing your job duties.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Can I work more than one Federal Work Study job simultaneously?</strong></span></h4>
<p>No. You can only be employed in one FWS job at a time.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Can I move money from one work-study job to another after I've accepted each of them?</strong></span></h4>
<p>Any money you have not earned during your initial job is available to be earned in your next job opportunity.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Can I work after the last day of the Spring semester?</strong></span></h4>
<p>There are no Federal Work Study-subsidized jobs between the last day of the Spring semester and July 1st. If the employer allows you to work during that time, they are responsible for 100% of your earnings.</p>
<p>If you have Federal Work Study as part of your Financial Aid for the following Fall semester, the employer can repost/register the job through Handshake to reflect the new program year via a July 1st or later begin date and refer you to the position. This is because you receive a new financial aid package each year and possibly Federal Work Study as part of that package. The new offer needs to be activated, applied to the specific job, and tracked for our federal audits.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>What happens on the last day of the Spring semester?</strong></span></h4>
<p>The Federal Work Study program year is over. Any Federal Work Study offer amount left unearned is gone. It does not carry over into your financial aid for the next program year.</p></p>

                                        <p class="body2"><h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>How much does a work-study job pay?</strong></span></h4>
<p>From minimum wage to upwards of more than $20/hour (usually graduate student positions). Employers set the rate and the highly skilled/greater responsibility jobs tend to pay more.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>If I don't work, do I have to give the money back?</strong> </span></h4>
<p>Since you don't receive the money until you earn it, and you can't earn it without having a work study job, there is no money to give back. The answer is "no."</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Do my earnings go to my student account?</strong></span></h4>
<p>No, work study earnings are not disbursed to your student account and do not reduce your tuition bills. They are earned wages through employment that you&#8217;ll receive in the form of a paycheck. You will need to budget earnings towards your college costs.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Who do I contact to be set-up on payroll?</strong></span></h4>
<p>Contact the Human Resources administrator where you work. If unsure, ask your supervisor. Note: Payroll does not go through the Work Study Office; students are paid directly by their department/employer.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>What do I need to be set-up on the Cal Poly Pomona Payroll?</strong></span></h4>
<p>To be set up in payroll, you will be asked to complete your New Hire Paperwork using DocuSign, an electronic system where you will sign most of the paperwork online. You will also need to provide <a href="https://www.cpp.edu/eoda-hr/student-employment/docs/sa_appt_offer_ay2324.pdf"><strong>two forms of ID (PDF)</strong></a> in order to complete the Federal (I-9) Employment Eligibility Verification Form which you must do in person, after completing the paperwork. Your supervisor can assist you with questions about coordinating this and will work with Berkeley Regional Services (BRS) for these actions.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>How do I get paid and how often?</strong> </span></h4>
<p>You are paid once per month, according to the <a href="https://www.cpp.edu/eoda-hr/departments/payroll/calendars/student-assistant-pay-schedule.shtml">Student Assistant Payroll Calendar.</a></p>
<ul style="list-style-type: square;">
<li>Paychecks may be picked up at your department on the designated PAYDAYS. Paychecks will be mailed to students working <span style="text-decoration: underline;">off-campus</span>.</li>
<li>Students are required to present their Bronco Access Card or Driver's License when picking up their paychecks.</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Are my work-study earnings taxable?</strong> </span></h4>
<p>Yes, generally work study earnings are taxable, just like regular work earnings.</p>
<p>Note that off-campus employers are obligated to deduct FICA taxes. You will be required to complete a W-4 Employee Withholding Allowance Certificate when you start working, which will determine how much income tax is withheld from your work-study earnings.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>How do I receive my W-2 form?</strong></span></h4>
<p>The Form W-2 reflects wages paid by warrants/direct deposit payments that were issued to you during the tax year (January 1 through December 31), regardless of the pay period in which you earned the wages.</p>
<p>The W-2 form is issued once a year and is mailed by the State Controller's Office directly to the mailing address you have on file with Payroll Services. W-2's are mailed to your mailing address normally by January 20th of each year. Undeliverable W-2 forms are returned to Payroll Services.</p>
<p>You can read more about distribution of the W-2 form on the Cal Poly Pomona <a href="https://www.cpp.edu/eoda-hr/departments/payroll/w-2/index.shtml">Payroll Services</a> website.</p></p>

                                        <p class="body2"><h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Are there any restrictions with work-study jobs?</strong> </span></h4>
<p>Yes, federal regulations restrict work-study from being used to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Displace a regularly-hired or budgeted employee - for instance, we can work with for-profit companies, but the job needs to be created for a student and be directly related to a student's major/career goals. Summer internships are great for that. On the other hand, we couldn't allow an employer to hire the subsidized work study student as part of its counter help, because the employer would be displacing a regularly hired/budgeted employee.</li>
<li>Promote organizations that restrict membership, such as a union.</li>
<li>Campaign for a political candidate or issue.</li>
<li>Lobby legislatures.</li>
<li>Recruit members for a religious organization or construct/maintain a religious building.</li>
<li>Work outside the United States unless with a branch of the campus or the U.S. government (an embassy, for example).</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h4><span style="font-size: 14pt;"><strong>Can any employer be a work-study employer?</strong> </span></h4>
<p>Any campus employer can be a work study employer and we also work with off-campus employers who set-up a contract with us.</p></p>
