    function toggleDropdown(menu) {
        if (menu.hasClass("hidden")) {
            menu.removeClass("hidden");
            menu.addClass("show");
            
            //add click listener to check for clicks outside of the nav
            //https://stackoverflow.com/questions/152975/how-do-i-detect-a-click-outside-an-element/3028037#3028037
            $(document).click(function(event) { 
                var $target = $(event.target);
                if(!$target.closest('#subNavContainer').length) {
                    closeDropdown(menu);
                }
            });
            
            //add listener for escape key
            $(document).on('keydown', function(event) {
                if (event.key == "Escape") {
                    closeDropdown(menu);
            }});
        } else {
            menu.removeClass("show");
            menu.addClass("hidden");
        }
    }

            }
        })

                                        <p class="body2"><p>The Internal Revenue Service today warned of an ongoing IRS-impersonation scam that appears to primarily target educational institutions, include students and staff who have ".edu" email addresses.</p>
<p>The phishing emails appear to target university and college students from both public and private, profit and non-profit institutions.</p>
<p>The suspect emails display the IRS logo and use various subject lines such as "Tax Refund Payment" or "Recalculation of your tax refund payment." It asks people to click a link and submit a form to claim their refund.</p>

For security reasons, save the email using "save as" and then send that attachment to <a href="mailto:phishing@irs.gov">phishing@irs.gov</a> or forward the email as an attachment to <a href="mailto:phishing@irs.gov">phishing@irs.gov</a>.                                     <button aria-expanded="false" aria-label="Emergency Broadband Program for Pell Grant Recipients" class="accordion__header" tabindex="0">
                                        <h3 class="h6__proxima">Emergency Broadband Program for Pell Grant Recipients</h3>
                                        <div class="accordion__item--right">
                                            <svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" class="accordion-chevron" fill="none" height="17" viewBox="0 0 29 17" width="29">

                                        <p class="body2"><p><em>Students can save money on internet service!</em></p>
<p><strong>May 5th, 2021:</strong>&#160; Federal Pell Grant recipients are one of several groups of individuals who qualify for the <a href="https://getemergencybroadband.org/"><strong>Emergency Broadband Benefit Program</strong></a>.&#160; This Federal Communications Commission (FCC) program provides a temporary discount on monthly broadband bills for qualifying households.</p>
<h4><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong>Benefits</strong></span></h4>
<p>The Emergency Broadband Benefit will provide:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>a discount of up to $50 per month</strong> for broadband services purchased by eligible households and up to $75 per month for households located on Tribal lands.</li>
<li><strong>a one-time maximum discount of $100 for purchase of a desk, laptop or tablet computer </strong>from participating providers; however, consumers must contribute $10-$50 toward the actual purchase price.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Emergency Broadband Benefit is limited to one monthly service discount and one device discount per household.</p>
<h4><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong>Who Is Eligible?</strong></span></h4>
<p>The Emergency Broadband Benefit Program is open to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Federal Pell Grant recipients</li>
<li>Households with students receiving free and reduced-price lunch or school breakfast</li>
<li>Those who have lost jobs and seen their income reduced in the last year due to furloughs</li>
<li>Households that participate in an existing low-income or pandemic relief program offered by a broadband provider</li>
<li>Lifeline subscribers, including those who are on Medicaid or accept SNAP benefits</li>
</ul>
<p>Access a full list of eligibility criteria at <a href="https://getemergencybroadband.org/do-i-qualify/">getemergencybroadband.org/do-i-qualify/</a>.</p>
<h4><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong>When Can I Sign Up for the Benefit?</strong></span></h4>
<p>Starting on May 12, 2021, eligible households will be able to enroll in the program to receive a monthly discount off the cost of broadband service from an approved provider.&#160; The program will end when the fund runs out of money, or six months after the Department of Health and Human Services declares an end to the COVID-19 health emergency, whichever comes first.</p>
<p>The agency is urging students to familiarize themselves with the&#160;<a href="https://getemergencybroadband.org/how-to-apply/">registration process</a> as well as <a href="https://getemergencybroadband.org/how-to-apply/show-you-qualify/">required documentation</a> so that, come May 12, they&#8217;ll be able to enroll and work through the FCC verification process as soon as the program is live.</p>
<p>2020-2021 Cal Poly Pomona Pell Grant recipients will receive an email from the Office of Financial Aid &amp; Scholarships documenting their Pell Grant award.&#160; This can be used for program eligibility documentation.&#160;&#160;</p>
<h4><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong>How do I enroll?</strong></span></h4>
<p>Eligible households can enroll through an <a href="https://www.fcc.gov/emergency-broadband-benefit-providers#California">approved provider</a> or by visiting the <a href="https://getemergencybroadband.org">Emergency Broadband Benefit program&#8217;s website</a>.</p>
<h4><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong>For more information</strong></span></h4>
<p>To learn more about the program, visit the <a href="https://www.fcc.gov/broadbandbenefit">FCC&#8217;s Emergency Broadband Benefit page</a>.</p></p>

                                                                </header>
                            </div>
                            <div class="row">
                                <div class="body2 text-start col-12 col-sm-8"><h2><span style="font-size: 18pt;"><strong>Information About Financial Aid Scams</strong></span></h2>
<p>Need money for college? Doesn't everybody? With tuition bills skyrocketing, and room and board going through the roof, students and their families are looking for creative ways to finance a college education. Unfortunately, in their efforts to pay the bills, many of them are falling prey to scholarship and financial aid scams.</p>
<p>The most critical feature in identifying a scam whether there is a charge applied for a service. It is common for a needy family to pay $1,000 or more for assistance in obtaining financial aid. Such costs are inappropriate and a scam. If there is a promise of an improved amount or type of financial aid award the family can expect the offer is most likely a scam. It&#8217;s just not possible to guarantee an improved level of grant or scholarship support.</p>
<p>There are some legitimate companies that can assist in filing our forms and provide a legitimate level of assistance. Such costs should be relevant and appropriate for the level of service provided.</p>
<p>Virtually every financial office has professionals that provide assistance free of charge to families. Families can go to local college and sit down with a financial aid professional at no charge for help completing the required documents.</p>
<p>Learn more about avoiding scams on the <a href="https://studentaid.gov/resources/scams">Federal Student Aid website</a>.</p></div>

<p>The Federal Trade Commission recently issued a <strong> <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/blog/2020/12/hey-college-students-have-you-seen-scam">consumer alert</a> </strong>about an emerging scam aimed at college students looking for a job. Cal Poly Pomona student employment information is typically only available via <a href="https://my.cpp.edu/">MyCPP</a> or Handshake. Students interested in looking for a job at Cal Poly Pomona are encouraged to be cautious of suspicious emails received from outside organizations.</p>
<h3><a href="https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/press-releases/2020/11/ftc-warns-frank-financial-aid-stop-potentially-misleading"> <strong> <span style="font-size: 14pt;">November 2020 - FTC Issues Warning to Frank Financial Aid</span> </strong> </a></h3>
<p><span style="font-size: 14pt;"> </span>As part of its ongoing effort to monitor the marketplace for questionable claims arising from the COVID pandemic, <a href="https://ftc.gov/news-events/press-releases/2020/11/ftc-warns-frank-financial-aid-stop-potentially-misleading">FTC staff just sent a warning letter to New York-based Frank Financial Aid</a>, raising concerns about representations regarding CARES Act grants, as well as a cash advance product the company is advertising.</p>
<p>What has FTC staff concerned? Some potentially misleading claims on Frank&#8217;s website. One fundamental fact to keep in mind is that for assistance through the Department of Education&#8217;s Higher Education Emergency Relief Fund created by the CARES Act, the Department has made it clear that each school has its own unique application process and &#8220;decides the criteria for qualified students to receive a grant, the grant amount, and how and when the grant will be disbursed (paid out) to students&#8221;</p>

<p>The Federal Trade Commission recently issued a <strong> <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/blog/2020/05/covid-19-scams-targeting-college-students">consumer alert</a> </strong> about an emerging scam aimed at college students and COVID-19 emergency grants.&#160;</p>
<p>Before you click on a link in any email or share any of your sensitive information:</p>
<ul>
<li>Check it out. If you have concerns about an email, contact the sender directly. Look up their phone number or website yourself. Don&#8217;t click on a link. That way, you&#8217;ll know you&#8217;re not about to call a scammer or follow a link that will download <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0011-malware">malware</a>.</li>
<li>Take a closer look. While some phishing emails look completely legit, bad grammar and spelling can be a tip-off to phishing. Another clue that the email is not really from your school: they use the wrong department name. In one example we&#8217;ve seen, the scammers called themselves the Financial Dept or stated the email was from &#8220;the Financial Aid.&#8221;</li>
</ul>
